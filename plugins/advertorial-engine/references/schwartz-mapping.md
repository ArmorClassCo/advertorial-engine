# Schwartz Mapping — sophistication × awareness → build decisions

Self-contained. Based on Eugene Schwartz's Breakthrough Advertising frameworks,
operationalized from a corpus of proven pages (ads-weighted evidence).

## Market sophistication — how burned is the market?

- **Stage 1** — first claim in a virgin market. Nobody has heard the promise.
- **Stage 2** — claim enlargement: bigger, faster, more.
- **Stage 3** — claims stop working; the market wants a NEW MECHANISM (how it
  works, not what it does).
- **Stage 4** — mechanisms compete: enlarged/differentiated mechanism, plus the
  mechanism-of-failure of rivals ("why everything you tried couldn't work").
- **Stage 5** — the market disbelieves everything. Claims are dead. What works:
  identification (who the buyer IS), experience, named-pattern diagnosis,
  honesty theater (damaging admissions, honest scorecards), and proof density.

**How to read the stage (evidence, not vibes):** count the mechanisms the
prospect has already seen. If every competitor leads with a mechanism and
"doesn't work like the others" is itself a cliché → stage 4–5. If the category
is new to this audience → stage 1–3. Direct evidence: competitor angle maps,
saturation of claims in the ad library, buyer language ("I've tried everything").

## Prospect awareness — what does the CLICK already know?

- **Unaware** — doesn't know the problem has a name.
- **Problem-aware** — feels it daily; doesn't know solutions exist.
- **Solution-aware** — knows the category; hasn't picked a product.
- **Product-aware** — knows THIS product (or the incumbent competitor).
- **Most-aware** — wants it; waiting on the deal.

The TRAFFIC sets awareness, not the market: the same product needs different
pages per ad angle. When unknown, assume problem-aware.

## The archetype selector

| awareness ↓ / stage → | 1–2 (virgin) | 3 (new mechanism) | 4 (mechanism war) | 5 (burned) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| unaware | direct-promise advertorial | ADV-2 story (diagnosis-first) | ADV-3 mechanism editorial | ADV-3 named-pattern (invent the diagnosis) |
| problem | condition-promise page | ADV-2 story | LIS-1 trending listicle / ADV-3 | LIS-4 root-cause; taxonomy reframe; ADV-2 with absolution |
| solution | promise + proof | ADV-2/3 mechanism reveal | LIS-2 comparison / ADV-3 + interception kicker | LIS-2 with damaging admission; ADV-4 identity editorial |
| product | offer page | offer + mechanism recap | LIS-2 conquest vs the incumbent | ADV-1 hybrid + honesty theater |
| most | plain offer | offer + deal frame | ADV-1 + deeper discount | ADV-1 + bundle/AOV + identity CTA verbs |

## Lead type by stage

- Stage 1–2: **promise lead** — the claim, biggest believable version.
- Stage 3: **mechanism lead** — the how, named.
- Stage 4: **differentiated-mechanism lead** + mechanism-of-failure of rivals.
- Stage 5: **identification / story / named-pattern lead** — NEVER a bigger
  claim. Coin a term for the reader's experience and diagnose it.

## Proof density by stage (scale with skepticism)

- Stage ≤3: 3–5 proof elements (reviews count, one stat, guarantee).
- Stage 4: 6–9 (add trial stats with decimals, comparison table, expert).
- Stage 5: 10+ (add damaging admission, honest scorecards, founder letter,
  segment-matched testimonials, expectation timeline, third-party-witness
  proof — someone ELSE noticed the change).

## CTA depth by awareness

- Most/product-aware: CTA in the hero (top 5–10% of words) and under every
  proof unit; discount-forward button text.
- Solution-aware: first CTA after the mechanism (~10–30%), or mid-list breaks;
  soft verbs ("See if it's available") before hard verbs ("Claim 40% off").
- Problem/unaware: CTA only after the education is complete (≥40%; post-list
  for listicles). Selling early to cold traffic kills belief.

## Persona by format (corpus rule)

- Advertorials: brand or customer narrator. No byline, no date line.
- Listicles: named persona byline + fresh "Last Updated" date — the editorial
  frame is what a list is FOR.
- "Advertisement"/"Advertorial" disclosure labels: a compliance decision that
  belongs to the operator and placement, not to the format. Omit by default;
  add when the client's channel requires.

## Stage-mismatch failure modes (the corpus's proven losers)

1. Stage-3 claim inflation on a stage-5 market ("look decades younger",
   "trending!") — the lowest-spend pages in the corpus made exactly this error
   on otherwise-proven skeletons.
2. Skipping the identification ramp for burned audiences — the trimmed version
   of a conquest page lost to the long version.
3. Editorial disguise without editorial substance — a byline on a page that
   reads like an ad satisfies nobody; commit to the frame or drop it.
